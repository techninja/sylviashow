Sylvia's Super-Awesome Maker Show! - build http://sylviashow.com/tags/build en BAMF Demo: Arduino Laser Tripwire http://sylviashow.com/blog/techninja/2011/05/22/bamf-demo-arduino-laser-tripwire <div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded"><p>At Bay Area Maker Faire (BAMF) 2011 (and now at Maker Faire KC) one of our demos is how to build your very own laser tripwire to help guard your room!</p> <p>Inspired by action_owl's version at <a href="http://www.instructables.com/id/Twittering-Laser-Tripwire-with-Webcam-Capture/">instructables</a>, Sylvia and I made up our very own version with audio feedback for calibration, and authentic alarm sounds!</p> <p><a href="http://sylviashow.com/sites/default/files/post-images/Dadmin/tripwire_0.JPG" class="yoxview_thumbnail"><img class="post-img-c" typeof="foaf:Image" src="http://sylviashow.com/sites/default/files/styles/blog_large/public/post-images/Dadmin/tripwire_0.JPG?itok=DYqMXavk" alt="The ALTAlarm inside a mint tin" title="The alarm consists of an arduino, a small red laser diode, an IR led (from a remote) and a speaker" /></a></p> <h2><a href="https://github.com/techninja/ALTAlarm/raw/master/Laser_Tripwire.pde">Get the code at Github to copy and paste into your Arduino IDE right here!</a></h2> <p>More instructions, build details and the like to be updated after our Demo today.</p> <hr /><p>Update: Alrighty, so our demo didn't go <em>quite</em> as planned, with our cheap laser diode falling to pieces <em>during</em> the presentation, but we did our best with what we had. We're quite sure that with more time we'd have had everything working great! Not that it matters to much, as there's always time for you to get out there and try it yourself!</p> <p>As the code explains hints at, for this build you only need a few things:</p> <ul><li>An Arduino (any version will do)</li> <li>A small cheap red laser diode (a small laser pointer will do)</li> <li>A small piece of mirror, or something else flat and reflective</li> <li>An infrared LED from a remote control (or something else to sense the light from the laser)</li> <li>and last but not least, a speaker (if you want a noisy alarm)</li> </ul><p>For the code as it stands, plug your laser's positive lead into pin 13, one lead of the speaker into pin 10, and the positive lead of the LED into analog pin 0. All the other leads go to ground. When powering up, the laser gets ~3volts from pin 13, which should be enough to light it and send it's photons off toward the mirror, where they will bounce off. If aimed correctly they should find their way back to the infrared LED.</p> <p>When infrared light hits the LED, it sends very tiny (but measurable) voltage back through to analog pin 0. On our LED, this was read at around 115, or about 0.5v. Without the laser, this came down below 100 reliably, so we set the alarm threshold at 95. If the LED's value stays above that number for more than 5 seconds, the alarm is considered "Armed" (it gives a nice beep every second to let you know, then a double beep when it's armed). If the beam is broken (and the LED value drops below the threshold), Woop woop! Alarm!</p> <p>A serial signal is sent at the same time, and if you have a program on a computer listening for this, you can trigger any action you want! Maybe a twitter message for every intrusion, or perhaps an email or text message silently alerting you!</p> <p>We may just want to go ahead and do a quick video on this cool little project in a bit, with all the bugs worked out this time :)</p> <p>Leave a comment if you've got questions! Thanks!</p> <p>Update: For our Maker Faire KC demo, we took out the middle man mirror and replaced the Arduino powered laser diode (which gave us problems galore!) with a simple laser pointer pointed directly at the LED. This is an invisible modification and doesn't require any code change, so you can make yours either way. </p> </div></div></div><div class="field field-name-field-tags field-type-taxonomy-term-reference field-label-above"> <h3 class="field-label">Tags&nbsp;</h3> <span class="field-items"> <a href="/tags/arduino" typeof="skos:Concept" property="rdfs:label skos:prefLabel" datatype="">Arduino</a>, <a href="/tags/build" typeof="skos:Concept" property="rdfs:label skos:prefLabel" datatype="">build</a>, <a href="/tags/cookies" typeof="skos:Concept" property="rdfs:label skos:prefLabel" datatype="">cookies</a>, <a href="/tags/laser" typeof="skos:Concept" property="rdfs:label skos:prefLabel" datatype="">laser</a>, <a href="/tags/away" typeof="skos:Concept" property="rdfs:label skos:prefLabel" datatype="">away</a>, <a href="/tags/maker-faire" typeof="skos:Concept" property="rdfs:label skos:prefLabel" datatype="">Maker Faire</a> </span> </div> Sun, 22 May 2011 17:16:11 +0000 TechNinja 29 at http://sylviashow.com http://sylviashow.com/blog/techninja/2011/05/22/bamf-demo-arduino-laser-tripwire#comments All about Mod Podge http://sylviashow.com/episodes/s1/e4/full/modpodge <div class="field field-name-field-ep-youtube field-type-link-field field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even"><iframe id="player" type="text/html" width="500" height="311" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/XwM_msMeDhY?enablejsapi=1&origin=sylviashow.com" frameborder="0"></iframe></div></div></div><div class="field field-name-field-ep-type field-type-list-text field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even">Full</div></div></div><div class="field field-name-body field-type-text-with-summary field-label-hidden"><div class="field-items"><div class="field-item even" property="content:encoded"><p>Mod Podge! The only thing left is to build the rocket body itself, for that we’ll need:</p> <ul><li>White school glue</li> <li>Thick legal size construction paper, or any other type of paper about that size</li> <li> A little strip of of cardboard, about 2 inches long</li> <li> A long cylinder or rod about 3/4” in diameter. Anything from a dowel to a broom handle</li> <li> A thin plastic drinking straw</li> <li> A file folder or fancy card stock</li> <li> Some stickers, markers and other crafty things to decorate the rocket with</li> <li> A paperclip (optional) to help secure the rocket engine</li> <li> And some not too sticky masking or painters tape</li> </ul><p>First, take your paper and wrap it nice and tight around your cylinder against a flat table while you glue. Use tape to secure it if needed. Use the card stock to cut out 3 to 4 fins, and glue them on evenly around the rocket body (or fuselage). You can add more if you want, just go crazy! The minimum number of fins is 4, but there's no real maximum (within reason). We added 4 more tiny ones above the main fins on one of our rockets.</p> <p>The entire assembly has to stay on the cylinder to dry completely overnight (otherwise there's a risk of the fuselage warping as it dries). Once it’s dry, roll up some card stock to make the nose cone and glue it on. Just be sure and trim off any excess nose cone paper to allow the launch rod to attach freely. Now draw a straight line down the fuselage and glue on two pieces of drinking straw for launch lugs.</p> <p>To make sure your rocket engine doesn’t fly out through your nose cone when it launches, roll the cardboard into a tight circle, coat it in glue and use an engine to push it into the fuselage just far enough to have a tiny lip of engine sticking out. To ensure the engine doesn’t go anywhere before launch, you can either glue in the engine, or use a straightened paper clip sandwiched in the fuselage and bent over to hold the engine. Even engines that are glued in should have the cardboard engine block installed.</p> <p>Once dried and decorated, your rocket is ready to launch! Find an open area away from trees and power lines, and set up your launch pad. Insert your ignitor with safety plug, attach the controller, get back to a safe distance, and launch! The greatest thing about these little rockets is they're so cheap to make, you won’t really mind the crash at the end. That’s it! Be sure and experiment with different fin shapes or nose cones. Have fun rocket scientists, and remember, get out there and make something!</p> <p>Songs used during this mini episode (all licensed under Creative Commons Share-alike v2.5 license):<br /> -The Wavers - Surfin' Mosquito</p> </div></div></div><div class="field field-name-field-ep-number field-type-number-integer field-label-inline clearfix"><h3 class="field-label">Ep.
